My CLion Experience


How we got here

When I started out with C++ in 2019, I was in the fortunate position that, while
we used NetBeans(!) in class to learn Java, my school gave out JetBrains
accounts with a student license to its pupils. To me this was a godsent, using
CLion over VisualStudio which I used before, was like changing worlds. It was
snappy, it was helpful (much better diagnostics), it forced me to learn CMake
over the weird MSVC project structure. It felt like the real deal. I was a big
fan of CLion.

Fast forward a few years, I have abandoned C++ for Rust for a while and VSCode
became my new best friend. I landed an internship for a C++ embedded developer
position. At work, we used CLion, and I was excited. I had fond memories of
CLion and felt right back at home. My experience however was a bit of a
different one. It felt a bit more sluggish, seemingly struggled with "simple"
tasks like "take me to the definition". Back then I shrugged it of. We had
Windows Laptops that were required for office things (Excel, Word, Teams) but
our development environment only worked on Linux. For this reason all of use
spent 95% of time in an Ubuntu virtual machine. I figured "surely all of this VM
overhead has a huge impact on performance and my experience".

Fast forward another couple of years and we are (almost) back in the present. In
that time I relapsed to Rust again, meanwhile having switched to Zed[0]. I
didn't like the direction VSCode (and especially Microsoft) was going in terms
of AI and found Zed to be a nice alternative for myself. I disabled all AI
features and had a fast, light, snappy, _native_ editor with Zed. Around the
same time I found myself having fun with programming terminal text editors[1]
and wanted to start over, make it more modern, use a proper event loop and add
scripting support. I remembered libuv[2] and decided to use C++. With Lua and
sol2[3] I had my stack planned and decided to go implement. Remembering CLion (
and abusing my university's JetBrains student license) I downloaded CLion again
and got started with my new project[4]…

The first impression

It began with the sluggish experience. While I used to love CLion for how snappy
it was while being a fully featured IDE, I felt a serious delay and lag in
everything I did. I would have thought my new Macbook with M4 processor should
be more than capable of just running an IDE. Whatever… It may not be as snappy
as I remember it but the tool and feature support is still top notch. The only
comparison I had was very little time spent in VSCode with the Microsoft C++
plugin.

I setup CMake, wrote a quick hello world. Compile, run, "Hello, World!", ground
breaking. More code, format, sav… Wait a minute. Why does my cursor keep moving

something? It happened again… I don't want to bother a lot with the formatting
while getting my ideas out into code, but I want to look at them nicely
formatted once they _are_ out. This breaks my flow in a weird way since my
workflow usually consists of writing code, formatting, and saving. Whatever,
I'll get used to this. Sometimes however I forget and don't notice that the
cursor moved, frequently resulting in something like the following:

this->peekForward();#ifndef CURSOR_HPP_

_Sigh_. I look at the settings. Wait what? What's the difference between 'Format
File' and 'Format Document'? I still don't know. _What_ I know however is, that
they don't have the same weird behaviour. No, no. 'Format Document' is even
worse. It _sometimes_ moves the cursor to the beginning of the file, and
_sometimes_ moves it to the end of the file. I tried my best to find out what
condition triggers which, but I couldn't. And I have not yet told the best (
worst) thing. It does not only move the cursor, it also moves THE ~~ROOK~~
VIEWPORT!! Not only breaks it my flow, it also forces me to SCROLL BACK to where
I was, to click my cursor to where I want to continue editing. I switched back
to 'Format File'…

It gets worse

Maybe I have too high standards towards an editor. I also have high standards
towards how the formatted code should look. I like my includes ordered and
sorted, adequat whitespace, a strict colum limit, etc… For those purposes I
configured CLions inbuilt formatter exactly the way I want it. Perfect, how
convenient, I get all the formatting I want, without having to learn how .clang-
format works. I save my preferences, go to my file. Lots of small yellow
squiggly lines: "style error". I Hover my cursor over one of them, wait for the
small message overlay, click "apply suggestion". Wait, this is not the way I
want it. _Undo_. "Are you sure you want to undo?", yes. "Do you want to undo?",
yes. "Undo?", …yes. Why are there so many dialogues? I fix my settings. My
cursor is _somewhere_ in my file. I hover with my mouse over another suggestion.
The overlay is for where my cursor is… Not where my mouse hovers, where my
_cursor_ hovers. I don't even know. Getting used to that, at least now
everything is well formatted. Except, it doesn't correctly order includes… And
the automatic includes while writing are unreliable as well. _Sigh_.

Time for some rapid-fire issues I came across. Types in partial words are shown.

It highlights 'Ren' in 'Render' as a typo for 'Run'. Search and static analysis
doesn't exclude git-excluded files, even when specifically setting it to respect
.gitignore. Renaming struct variables searches SYSTEM HEADERS (headers that
CLion _knows_ are system headers and not part of the project) for occurances.
Rename in the right-click menu shows the path with backslashes, on macOS… And by
far the worst offender. By default, pairing opening brackets _on existing
brackets_ is enabled. If you have code like the following

int square(int x) {
    return x * x;
}


and put your cursor after the opening '{', then press enter _it inserts a
closing '}'_… Why is that the default. You can even disable it. They made the
conscious decision to keep this as a default. With existing brackets. Why?

The power of third-party tools


I could handle the sluggishnes. I could handle the cursor moving when
formatting. I could even handle search ignoring my .gitignore when I
specifically tell it to respect it. But this default. I couldn't. I quit CLion.
And I don't wish to use it in the future anymore. The last thing I used CLion
for was to export my config to a .clang-format file (which is a very nice
feature), then I uninstalled it. Zed with a strict clang-format[5] config and
some additional clangd[6] and clang-tidy[7] configs was just as powerful if not
even more powerful than CLion. Even the static analysis, that I always found so
much better than anywhere else in CLion, felt better and more precise using
clangd and clang-tidy. This was also when I realised, I don't want to pidgeon-
hole myself into some IDE that does it all for me. Because once this changes in
a way I don't like, I lose everything. Third-party tools like the llvm clang
suite with an editor that supports external tools (mainly the Language Server
Protocol[8]) and running terminal commands on a keybind do the job just as well.

Thank you for reading all of this :3 This was my very first post! You're welcome
to mail me to share your own experiences or thoughts.
